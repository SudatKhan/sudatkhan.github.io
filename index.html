Research

Throughout the Fall 2021 semester (and continuing into the Spring 2022 semester) I was able to start independent research under the mentorship of Dr. Philip J. Armitage. During this time I gained the ability to understand astrophysics research papers in topics such as Circumplanetary Disk Formation and Tidal Disruptions events caused by Black Hole binaries. I was also able to learn about smoothed particle hydrodynamics (SPH), a meshfree computational method based on the Lagrangian formulation of classical mechanics that can solve the equations of fluid dynamics. In this method, a fluid is treated as a collection of individual point mass particles. The Euler equation for an ideal fluid would be: \[\rho \frac{d \vec{v}}{dt} = - \vec{\nabla} P + \vec{F}_{ext} \] In particular, I taught myself a Fortran SPH code called PHANTOM (developed by Dr. Daniel J. Price) which can create neat simulations of sedov blast waves (a uniform density supernovae blast) and tidal disruption events.


Currently, I am working with Dr. Philip J. Armitage and PhD student Cristiano Longarini (University of Milan) on simulating Jupiter-mass planet collisions (which are modeled using polytropes), and how the debris from those collisions form accretion disks around a central star. Simulating the collisions required the creation of specific algorithm files (called "moddumps" in PHANTOM) written in Fortran which Cristiano Longarini helped develop.
